02 — The Margin

Why wider is better

The margin is the distance between the two parallel support hyperplanes. SVM maximises this — the Perceptron ignores it entirely.

Margin geometry
SVM primal objectiveminimise ½‖w‖² s.t. yᵢ(w·xᵢ + b) ≥ 1
Margin widthγ = 2 / ‖w‖
Perceptron stops here
Once every training point is correctly classified, learning halts — regardless of how close the boundary is to any point.
SVM finds the maximum margin
SVM solves a convex QP that explicitly maximises the perpendicular gap between the two closest parallel margin lines.
Only support vectors matter
The boundary is defined entirely by the handful of points on the margin edge. Remove all other points — boundary unchanged.
VC theory guarantee
A larger margin directly reduces the generalisation error bound — this is the theoretical reason why SVM works.

04 — Intuition

The student analogy

Perceptron

Like a student who memorises the exact answers to every training question — even the flawed ones. When the exam changes slightly, they stumble.

→ Overfits boundary details
SVM

Like a student who learns the general rule and ignores noise. The margin is the buffer they keep — room to be wrong on small perturbations without misclassifying.

→ Learns structural rule
Key insight

SVM only cares about points on the margin edge. Points already far from the boundary contribute nothing — making the model robust to their small movements.
